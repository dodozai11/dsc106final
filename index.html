Where Does Carbon Go — and What Does It Cost Us?

Exploring Earth's carbon cycle under two futures: a moderate emissions path SSP2-4.5 vs. a high-emissions path SSP5-8.5

Atmospheric CO₂ Mass

The Pre-Industrial Baseline

In the many years prior to the Industrial Revolution, atmospheric carbon remained consistently stable, especially in relation to the natural carbon cycle where oceans absorbed carbon and forests were releasing it.

20th Century Acceleration

Post-WWII economic expansion supercharged emissions. Cars, planes, factories. The modern world became heavily reliant on fossil fuels. By 2000, CO₂ levels were higher than at any point in human history.

Carbon Emissions Today

We are here. The historical record ends and the projections begin. The path forward is determined by Shared Socioeconomic Pathways (SSPs), which are climate scenarios developed to model different possible futures based on policy and societal choices.

The Fork in the Road

Under SSP2-4.5, emissions peak and begin to decline as clean energy scales up. Under SSP5-8.5, fossil fuel use accelerates unchecked. The two paths diverge sharply after 2030.

Two Very Different Futures

By 2100, the gap between the two scenarios is enormous. The choice between these futures is not a matter of technology — it is a matter of political and economic will.

Global Mean Temperature

Near-surface air temperature (°C). Rising CO₂ drives warming — the gap between scenarios widens after 2050.

Ocean CO₂ Uptake

Mean flux of CO₂ from atmosphere into ocean (g m⁻² yr⁻¹). The ocean acts as a carbon sink — but its capacity weakens under high emissions.

Global Land Carbon Distribution

Spatial distribution of terrestrial carbon storage (kg m⁻²). Scroll to zoom, drag to pan, hover for values.

About This Visualization

We updated the webpage from prior projects, where there was previously an interactive map showcasing land carbon distribution and interactive graphs of ocean CO₂ uptake and mean temperature. We have now added a scrollytelling visualization of Earth's carbon cycle using CMIP6 climate data. For this narrative, we show descriptions of historically and projected significant time periods on the right. The main graph is a sticky CO₂ timeseries that progressively reveals historical data then transitions to future scenarios as the user scrolls down.

The most challenging part of the project will be designing a strong hook for the beginning with author-driven visuals. Carbon emissions is a very common topic, so it may be difficult to create a compelling visualization that makes viewers emotionally resonant with the data. We will consider using different opening visuals and strong annotations that connect real-world events to what readers already care about. Another challenge will be coordinating different interactions such as chart updating and hover information while remaining consistent with the scrolling experience.
